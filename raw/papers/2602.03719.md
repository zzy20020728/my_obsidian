Abstract
Agentic reinforcement learning has enabled large language models to perform complex multi-turn planning and tool use. However, learning in long-horizon settings remains challenging due to sparse, trajectory-level outcome rewards. While prior tree-based methods attempt to mitigate this issue, they often suffer from high variance and computational inefficiency. Through empirical analysis of search agents, We identify a common pattern: performance diverges mainly due to decisions near the tail. Motivated by this observation, we propose Branching Relative Policy Optimization (BranPO), a value-free method that provides step-level contrastive supervision without dense rewards. BranPO truncates trajectories near the tail and resamples alternative continuations to construct contrastive suffixes over shared prefixes, reducing credit ambiguity in long-horizon rollouts. To further boost efficiency and stabilize training, we introduce difficulty-aware branch sampling to adapt branching frequency across tasks, and redundant step masking to suppress uninformative actions. Extensive experiments on various question answering benchmarks demonstrate that BranPO consistently outperforms strong baselines, achieving significant accuracy gains on long-horizon tasks without increasing the overall training budget. 4.2
Difficulty-Aware Branch Sampling
Figure 3
:
Pass@K results of SFT-trained Qwen2.5 and Qwen3 models with partial resampling at the trajectory tail.
Building directly on this observation, we note that under trajectory-level supervision, downstream failures at late-stage decisions are uniformly propagated to all preceding actions. Consequently, early correct decisions may still be penalized due to errors in later stages, leading to inaccurate credit assignment.
To address this issue, we introduce
branch sampling
. Given an initial rollout, we truncate the trajectory at a late turn and resample only the remaining suffix while keeping the prefix fixed. This allows us to evaluate whether earlier actions are intrinsically incorrect or merely affected by subsequent errors. By focusing on the trajectory tail, branch sampling concentrates supervision on high-uncertainty decisions.
To avoid uninformative samples, we retain only
contrastive branches
. Specifically, if the original rollout is incorrect, we search for a correct continuation; if it is correct, we instead search for an incorrect one. If the resampled continuation yields the same reward, we discard it and retain only the original trajectory. Only branches whose outcomes differ from the original trajectory are used for training, ensuring that each branch provides meaningful contrastive signal.
A fixed branching budget is inefficient across tasks of varying difficulty: easy instances rarely yield informative alternatives, while harder ones require deeper exploration. As illustrated in Figure
2
, BranPO therefore adopts a
difficulty-aware branch sampling
strategy, where task difficulty is inferred from group-level accuracy after the initial rollout. Specifically, correct trajectories from easy tasks receive simple branching as described above, whereas hard tasks or incorrect trajectories trigger recursive branching to explore diverse contrastive suffixes. This adaptive allocation concentrates computation on challenging instances where alternative continuations are most informative, while avoiding unnecessary branching on trivial cases, thereby improving both computational and sample efficiency. The full algorithm is summarized with additional details in Appendix
A.1

Redundant tool usage is a common failure mode in multi-turn search agents

Conclusion
In this work, we present Branching Relative Policy Optimization (BranPO), a value-free reinforcement learning (RL) framework designed to mitigate credit assignment ambiguity in long-horizon agentic tasks through branch sampling. Motivated by the observation that failure patterns occur mainly in late-stage steps, BranPO constructs step-level contrastive supervision via recursive truncation and resampling, transforming sparse trajectory-level feedback into preference-based learning signals with improved sample efficiency. By further integrating difficulty-aware branch sampling and Redundant Step Masking, BranPO enables efficient exploration while suppressing bias toward unnecessary continuation. Extensive experiments across seven multi-hop and single-hop QA benchmarks demonstrate that BranPO consistently outperforms strong baselines, delivering significant accuracy gains. Overall, our findings highlight a key insight for agentic RL: constructing step-level contrastive supervision provides a significantly more efficient training paradigm than relying solely on trajectory-level rewards.
Impact Statement
This paper presents Branching Relative Policy Optimization (BranPO), a training method designed to improve the efficiency of policy optimization and facilitate stronger model capabilities through more effective credit assignment. By reducing redundant rollouts and focusing supervision on informative decision points, BranPO enables more efficient training of search agent and reinforcement learning models.
At the same time, while BranPO improves training efficiency and optimization quality, it does not eliminate fundamental limitations of search-based reasoning, such as hallucinated intermediate steps or incorrect factual information in generated outputs. As a result, models trained with BranPO may still produce unverified or misleading information, and appropriate evaluation and deployment safeguards remain necessary.

RL Training
Our training framework is based on rLLM
(Tan
et al.
,
2025
)
, an agentic RL training system built on veRL
(Sheng
et al.
,

•
Token-in-token-out.
In multi-turn training, each round’s prompt includes the interaction history from previous rounds. In the original rLLM implementation, the prompt is re-encoded by the tokenizer at every turn. We observe that the re-tokenized sequence may not exactly match the tokens previously generated by the LLM, introducing off-policy effects that harm training stability. To address this issue, we cache the token IDs produced by the LLM at each turn and concatenate them with the existing prompt tokens and encoded environment tokens to form the next-turn prompt, avoiding repeated re-encoding and decoding.
•
Rollout Correction.
This technique mitigates off-policy effects caused by distributional discrepancies between the inference engine and the training engine. We apply truncated token-level importance sampling to reduce this mismatch

et al.

Evaluation Details
Baselines
We compare three different reinforcement learning methods:
•
GRPO
: This method is based on chain-based sampling. For each task, it samples
N
N
complete trajectories from scratch. The reward of each trajectory is uniformly distributed across every token at each step within the trajectory.
•
GiGPO
: This approach aggregates identical states (referred to as
anchor states
) across different trajectories in the rollout to compute step-level intra-group advantages.
•
Tree-GRPO
: By randomly truncating and branching trajectories to construct a tree-structured trajectory, this method estimates step-level values through a combination of intra-tree and inter-tree advantage computations.
Due to GiGPO’s distinct context management strategy, which treating each step as an independent response and computing gradients separately during updates, we repeatedly encountered out-of-memory errors during Stage 2 long-horizon training. Consequently, we only report and compare results from Stage 1 for GiGPO.

Despite these achievements, several limitations remain that merit further investigation. First, regarding search efficiency in long-horizon settings, redundant behaviors persist despite the mitigation provided by the Redundant Step Mask, indicating that more explicit regularization may be required to enforce strict efficiency. Second, the current scope of our evaluation is concentrated on information-seeking search agents. Expanding BranPO to broader agentic scenarios, such as general tool-use
(Patil
et al.
,
2025
)
, represents a critical direction for future research. Finally, while we successfully scaled training to 8 interactions, real-world deep research tasks often demand significantly longer horizons (e.g., 30+ interactions). Future work will focus on validating BranPO’s stability in these ultra-long-horizon regimes and assessing its scalability when applied to larger foundation models on more challenging reasoning tasks
(Wei
et al.
,
2025
)
.
