Abstract
In agentic search, large language models (LLMs) are trained to perform multi-turn retrieval and reasoning for complex tasks such as multi-hop question answering (QA). However, current search-based Reinforcement Learning (RL) methods suffer from two core limitations: expensive long-horizon rollouts are under-utilized during training, and supervision is typically available only at the final answer, resulting in severe reward sparsity. We present
P
refix-based
R
ollout reuse for
A
gentic search with
I
ntermediate
S
tep r
E
wards (PRAISE), a framework for improving both data efficiency and credit assignment in agentic search training. Given a complete search trajectory, PRAISE extracts prefix states at different search turns, elicits intermediate answers from them, and uses these prefixes both to construct additional training trajectories and to derive step-level rewards from performance differences across prefixes. Our method uses a single shared model for both search policy learning and prefix answer evaluation, enabling joint optimization without extra human annotations or a separate reward model. Experiments on multi-hop QA benchmarks show that PRAISE consistently improves performance over strong baselines.

Introduction
Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly moving beyond one-shot generation toward
agentic
problem solving, where a model interacts with external tools and environments over multiple steps to reason, plan, and act
Yao et al. (
2022
); Schick et al. (
2023
)
. Among these emerging paradigms,
agentic search
has become a particularly important framework for knowledge-intensive tasks such as multi-hop question answering (QA), deep research, and open-domain reasoning
Li et al. (
2025
); Jin et al. (
2025
); Song et al. (
2025
)
. Instead of relying on a fixed retrieval stage as in standard retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)
Lewis et al. (
2020
)
, agentic search enables a model to dynamically decide
when
to search,
what
to search for, and
how
to update its reasoning based on newly acquired evidence. This adaptive interaction with external information is crucial for solving problems whose answers cannot be obtained from parametric memory alone.
To improve search and reasoning in such systems, recent work increasingly applies reinforcement learning (RL) to agentic search. In this setting, the model executes a multi-turn search trajectory and is trained based on the quality of its final answer or overall task performance. A particularly important paradigm is reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR)
Lambert et al. (
2024
); Guo et al. (
2025
)
, where supervision comes from automatically checkable signals. By directly optimizing downstream task success, RLVR provides a natural training framework for agentic search. However, current search-based RL methods still face important limitations.
A central issue is that supervision in search-based RL is typically available only at the trajectory level. A single rollout often contains multiple rounds of reasoning, querying, reading, and search-state updates, making it substantially more costly than ordinary single-pass generation. Yet, in most existing approaches, the full trajectory is still treated as a single training instance, with learning signals derived primarily from the final answer. This leads to
poor data efficiency
Liu et al. (
2025
);
Huang and Wan
: although a rollout contains multiple informative intermediate search states, existing methods typically consume it as a single training instance rather than converting those intermediate states into additional learnable examples, limiting the value extracted from each expensive rollout. At the same time, it also results in
reward sparsity
: because supervision arrives only at the end of the trajectory, it becomes difficult to determine which intermediate search actions improved answer quality and which were redundant or harmful
Zheng et al. (
2025
); Cui et al. (
2025
)
. This combination of under-utilized intermediate information and delayed feedback makes long-horizon credit assignment especially challenging.
Prior work has attempted to address these issues from two largely separate directions. One line of research improves sample efficiency through replay buffers
Sun et al. (
2025
)
, trajectory reuse
Wang et al. (
2025b
)
, or off-policy updates
Chen et al. (
2025a
)
. Another line introduces denser supervision via process rewards
Cui et al. (
2025
)
, teacher-based evaluation
Xie et al. (
2026
)
, or intermediate annotations
Zhang et al. (
2025
); Zheng et al. (
2025
)
. However, these approaches remain limited in the agentic search setting. Existing reuse methods are typically centered around whole trajectories rather than the inherently compositional structure of multi-turn search. Meanwhile, process-supervision methods often rely on additional ingredients such as gold sub-questions, annotated evidence, separately trained reward models, or frozen teacher models, all of which introduce extra cost and may create mismatch between the evaluator and the evolving search policy.
In this paper, we ask a simple but important question:
how can we maximize the training value of each expensive search rollout?
To answer this question, we propose
PRAISE
, short for
P
refix-based
R
ollout reuse for
A
gentic search with
I
ntermediate
S
tep r
E
wards. The key idea is to exploit the natural prefix structure of search trajectories. Given a complete multi-turn rollout, we extract prefix states at different search turns and prompt the model to produce intermediate answers conditioned only on the information available up to each prefix. These prefix–answer pairs are then used in two complementary ways.
First, they provide
rollout reuse
. PRAISE converts a full rollout into multiple prefix-based training samples. In this way, intermediate search states that would otherwise be ignored are transformed into additional supervision, substantially increasing the amount of learnable signal extracted from each rollout. Second, they provide
intermediate step rewards
. By evaluating the quality of answers produced from different prefixes and comparing performance across adjacent prefixes, we estimate the marginal contribution of each search step. This converts sparse terminal supervision into fine-grained process rewards aligned with the search trajectory itself. Such reward densification leads to more informative training signals and improves long-horizon credit assignment.
A central design choice of PRAISE is that the same model is used both for search policy learning and for prefix answer evaluation. This shared-model formulation avoids the need for an additional reward model, thereby reducing system complexity and annotation cost. More importantly, it allows search policy improvement and prefix evaluation capability to co-evolve through joint optimization, which in turn improves the consistency between the acting policy and the source of intermediate supervision
Chen et al. (
2026b
,
a
)
.
We evaluate PRAISE on multi-hop QA benchmarks. Experimental results show that our method consistently outperforms strong search-based RL baselines. Further analysis shows that the method is robust to key hyperparameters and that joint optimization with a shared model performs substantially better than alternatives based on frozen evaluators.
Our contributions are summarized as follows:
•
We propose a prefix-based rollout reuse framework, transforming each expensive multi-turn search trajectory into multiple learnable prefix samples and improves rollout utilization.
•
We introduce a process reward construction method based on adjacent prefix gains to convert sparse terminal signals into fine-grained, step-aligned rewards.
•
We develop a joint optimization framework in which a single shared model performs both search decision making and prefix evaluation.
•
We demonstrate the effectiveness of PRAISE on multi-hop QA benchmarks, showing consistent improvements over strong baselines.

Related Work
Agentic Search and RL-based Training.
Recent advancements have focused on training LLMs as agentic search systems that interleave multi-step reasoning with external retrieval. Leveraging RL frameworks such as PPO
Schulman et al. (

)
or GRPO

)
, representative agentic search methods such as Search-R1
Jin et al. (
2025
)
and R1-Searcher
Song et al. (
2025
)
have demonstrated that such RL-based paradigms significantly bolster performance in complex search tasks. Parallel to these end-to-end agents, extensive research has investigated the optimization of specific search components, including planning
Chen et al. (
2025c
)
, query reformulation
Ma et al. (
2023
); Li et al. (
2024b
); Chen et al. (
2025b
)
, document selection
Ke et al. (
2024
); Li et al. (
2024a
)
, and answer generation
Yang et al. (
2025
)
. More recently, there is a growing emphasis on the joint optimization of these modular components to achieve superior global performance
Chen et al. (
2026b
,
a
)
.
Data Efficiency and Rollout Reuse.
Improving data efficiency remains a critical challenge in RL training for LLMs. One common strategy is data selection, for example by prioritizing questions of moderate difficulty for the current policy, which has been shown to be especially useful for GRPO-style training
Yu et al. (
2025
); Sun et al. (
2025
)
. Another strategy is trajectory reuse, where recent rollouts are reused across multiple optimization steps through replay buffers or relaxed on-policy updates
Sun et al. (
2025
); Chen et al. (
2025a
); Wang et al. (
2025b
)
. More recently, several works study prefix reuse to reduce redundant generation. PROS
Huang and Wan
reuses valuable intermediate prefixes to form augmented training inputs, while SPEC-RL
Liu et al. (
2025
)
treats previous rollouts as speculative drafts and resumes generation from the first unverifiable position. However, these methods are developed mainly for math or code reasoning, where “prefixes” are defined over token sequences rather than explicit search turns.
Process Supervision and Intermediate Rewards.
To address the reward sparsity in long-horizon reasoning, recent research has introduced denser supervision signals
Lightman et al. (
2023
); Wang et al. (
2025a
)
. StepSearch
Zheng et al. (
2025
)
constructs step-wise rewards using reference sub-questions, keywords, and documents, while ReasonRAG
Zhang et al. (
2025
)
estimates intermediate utility by rolling out from partial states and favoring shorter successful reasoning paths. TIPS
Xie et al. (
2026
)
and IGPO
Wang et al. (
2025a
)
define turn-level rewards through changes in answer likelihood under a teacher model or the policy model itself. More broadly, PRIME
Cui et al. (
2025
)
shows that dense process rewards can be learned online from outcome supervision alone, though its main focus is mathematical and coding reasoning. Unlike these, our PRAISE derives rewards directly from performance gains between adjacent search prefixes, eliminating the need for gold-standard annotations or external evaluators.

Overall, the full PRAISE model performs best across benchmarks, showing that the shared and jointly optimized evaluator is an important part of the framework. When joint optimization is removed and the policy model is directly reused as the evaluator without dedicated evaluator training, RL increasingly specializes the model toward the search objective, which can weaken its more general ability to assess prefix quality. As a result, the induced process rewards become less reliable, and this variant can even perform worse than removing the evaluator altogether. Frozen evaluators, including larger models, also underperform because they do not stay aligned with the evolving search policy during training, leading to inaccurate estimates of intermediate prefix quality. By contrast, joint optimization keeps the evaluator synchronized with the current search model, enabling more faithful step-level evaluation and higher-quality process rewards. Finally, removing process rewards or removing prefix evaluation also degrades performance, confirming that both intermediate supervision and prefix-based rollout reuse are important to the effectiveness of PRAISE.

5
Conclusion
We presented PRAISE, a prefix-based rollout reuse framework for agentic search training. By converting intermediate prefixes into additional training samples and deriving step-level rewards from adjacent prefix gains, PRAISE improves both rollout utilization and credit assignment. Built on a shared-model design, it jointly optimizes search and prefix evaluation within a unified PPO framework, without requiring extra annotations or a separate reward model. Experiments on multi-hop QA benchmarks demonstrate consistent gains over strong baselines.

A.1
Discussion
Why PPO instead of GRPO?
We adopt PPO rather than GRPO for this setting. GRPO is naturally designed for comparing a group of responses sampled from the same state. In multi-turn search, however, extending this paradigm would require grouped sampling at each turn and each intermediate state, leading to rapidly increasing rollout cost with the horizon. Although recent work such as IGPO extends a GRPO-style idea to multi-turn agents
Wang et al. (
2025a
)
, turns from different rollouts generally correspond to different states, making strict same-state grouping difficult to preserve. PPO is therefore a more natural choice for optimizing trajectories with token-level or turn-level rewards in our setting.
Why not use likelihood-based prefix scoring?
PRAISE also differs from likelihood-based prefix scoring methods such as TIPS
Xie et al. (

)
and IGPO
Wang et al. (
2025a
)
. Those methods estimate intermediate utility using the log-likelihood of the golden answer conditioned on a prefix. While this can serve as a zero-shot evaluation signal, it is less suitable for joint training. Once optimized directly, the objective becomes close to supervised fine-tuning on the reference answer, encouraging answer memorization rather than measuring the incremental value of newly retrieved evidence. By instead generating rollout-based prefix answers and scoring them with task reward, PRAISE preserves the meaning of process reward as the marginal gain brought by each search step.
