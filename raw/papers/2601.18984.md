Abstract
Reinforcement learning (RL) has emerged as a powerful framework for improving the reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs). However, most existing RL approaches rely on sparse outcome rewards, which fail to credit correct intermediate steps in partially successful solutions. Process reward models (PRMs) offer fine-grained step-level supervision, but their scores are often noisy and difficult to evaluate. As a result, recent PRM benchmarks focus on a more objective capability: detecting the first incorrect step in a reasoning path. However, this evaluation target is misaligned with how PRMs are typically used in RL, where their step-wise scores are treated as raw rewards to maximize. To bridge this gap, we propose Verifiable Prefix Policy Optimization (VPPO), which uses PRMs only to localize the first error during RL. Given an incorrect rollout, VPPO partitions the trajectory into a verified correct prefix and an erroneous suffix based on the first error, rewarding the former while applying targeted penalties only after the detected mistake. This design yields stable, interpretable learning signals and improves credit assignment. Across multiple reasoning benchmarks, VPPO consistently outperforms sparse-reward RL and prior PRM-guided baselines on both Pass@1 and Pass@K.

5
Conclusions
We present VPPO, a process-supervised RL method that turns noisy step-level PRM scores into a simple, reliable training signal: the location of the first mistake in a sampled reasoning chain. Using this mistake boundary, VPPO preserves and reinforces part of the
good prefix
, the maximal correct segment preceding the first error, via an additional reward, while explicitly penalizing only the erroneous suffix. This reallocation of learning signal fixes core failure mode of outcome-only RL, yielding updates that are both more targeted and stable. Across reasoning benchmarks, VPPO consistently improves Pass@1 and Pass@K results, outperforming sparse-reward baselines and prior PRM-enhanced methods. Future work could further investigate using first-error signals and good prefixes to help learning. For example, during RL, we can store good prefixes in a replay buffer and resample them to train the model to complete from diverse prefixes, which may improve generalization.
6
Limitation
The method prompts the policy to enumerate reasoning as “Step 1…Step 2…”, then defines steps by matching the literal marker. This creates a vulnerability: the model can “inflate” steps (split a large step into many micro-steps) to increase the rewarded token mass, causing overly verbose reasoning. The proposed shorten-prefix trick mitigates this, but the approach still fundamentally depends on this step format and parsing pipeline. The shorten-prefix scheme requires choosing a cut length
c
​
(
q
)

; the paper’s best-performing choice is setting

)

We use Qwen2.5-7B-MATH-PRM to identify the first incorrect step in a given response. Since Qwen2.5-7B-MATH-PRM outputs a score for every step, we need to select a threshold and define the predicted first incorrect step as the first step whose score falls below this threshold. To select a suitable threshold, we evaluate the model on ProcessBench
(Zheng

,
2024

During training, we only query the PRM for responses whose final answer is incorrect. Accordingly, for our analysis on ProcessBench, we restrict attention to trajectories whose final answer is incorrect, yielding
1700
1700

. We observe that Qwen2.5-7B-MATH-PRM tends to assign relatively high scores, so larger thresholds (up to
0.8
0.8

Our primary goal is to reward the correct prefix of the reasoning process. Consequently, it is benign if the PRM predicts the first incorrect step
earlier

later

Another safe failure mode is when the PRM does not identify any incorrect step for an incorrect response; in this case we assign zero reward to all tokens, ensuring that the incorrect step is not positively reinforced.
